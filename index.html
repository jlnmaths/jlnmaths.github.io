Research in Progress

On the Demand for Mental Models (with Katharina Momsen)
Abstract: We run a series of online experiments to investigate how decision-makers' inference from historical data is influenced by decision-support in the form of hints that point out patterns in historical data. In the experiments, participants face a prediction task while being exposed to two types of information: historical outcomes and hints about patterns in the historical dataset. Participants repeat the prediction task with the option to choose between additional data points and an additional hint. We investigate if decision-makers are prone to wishful thinking and whether they react more strongly to soft or strong hints. Moreover, we explore which hints are expected to be more convincing by our subjects and if this expectation is in line with actual behavior. We find that hints move assessments strongly and that the majority of subjects prefer hints over data. We find no evidence that agents react more strongly to patterns that induce higher anticipatory utility. Finally, we find that those hints that subjects expect to be more convincing indeed have stronger effects.
Don't Put All Your Legs in One Basket - Theory and Evidence on Coopetition in Road Cycling (with David Piazolo, R&R at European Economic Review) (Working Paper)
Abstract: Road cycling races, although won by individual riders, are a competition of teams. Riding behind other riders significantly reduces the energy required to hold a given speed. These races thus provide free-riding incentives. We introduce a game-theoretic framework of this strategic setup to analyze a team's winning probability in various race situations and to examine group characteristics facilitating coordination. We complement our theoretical results with an empirical analysis using data from more than 40 seasons of professional road cycling races. Our model suggests that asymmetry in rider strength or team strength within a group is favorable for group coordination. Also, adding teammates to competing groups is beneficial because it leads to strategic benefits, increasing the free-riding opportunities in both groups. We find empirical evidence that a teammate in a group behind has a positive impact on win probability, indicating that such an effect indeed exists.
